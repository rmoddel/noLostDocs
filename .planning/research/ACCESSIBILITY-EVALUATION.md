# Accessibility Evaluation Reference

## Why It Matters

Accessibility is not a fringe concern or a future nice-to-have. It is a baseline expectation for professional web work.

For product teams, agencies, and site operators, accessibility now carries practical legal, financial, and reputational risk. A site that fails basic accessibility checks is not just excluding users with disabilities. It is also signaling shallow technical execution.

## What Accessibility Measures

Accessibility measures how well people with disabilities can navigate, understand, and interact with a website. That includes:

- screen-reader users
- keyboard-only users
- low-vision users
- people relying on assistive technologies

For NoLostDocs, this matters directly because the product makes trust, recovery, and clarity part of the brand promise. Inaccessible flows undercut that promise.

## What To Look For In Reviews

When evaluating a website, an agency portfolio, or new product work, run basic accessibility checks instead of relying on visual polish alone.

Common signals to inspect:

- missing or weak alt text
- improper heading structure
- low color contrast
- missing form labels
- broken or misleading ARIA usage
- keyboard traps or non-operable controls
- illogical focus order

Not every automated flag is equally severe, but repeated errors, alerts, and contrast failures are meaningful red flags.

## What Accessibility Reveals

Accessibility is also a proxy for engineering discipline.

Clean semantic HTML, logical document structure, predictable interaction patterns, and keyboard operability do not happen by accident. They usually reflect deliberate implementation choices.

A portfolio or product surface with persistent accessibility failures suggests one or more of the following:

- shortcuts in delivery
- lack of awareness
- indifference to real users
- weak QA discipline

## Practical Review Method

Use lightweight accessibility auditing as part of normal site review:

1. Run an automated accessibility scan on the page or route.
2. Check the page with keyboard-only navigation.
3. Review heading order, form labeling, and focus behavior manually.
4. Treat repeat failures as engineering-quality issues, not just design issues.

## Bottom Line

Accessibility is not optional. It is a concrete measure of professionalism.

A site that is inclusive, well-structured, and usable is usually a sign of disciplined engineering. A site that ignores accessibility should be treated as a warning signal during product review, implementation review, or agency evaluation.
